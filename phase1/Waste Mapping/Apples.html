üîç Executive Summary: National Waste Assessment

This assessment quantifies Apple waste in Lebanon at a national scale. National apple waste is calculated from FAOSTAT production data combined with peer-reviewed loss ratios; values represent order-of-magnitude estimates. By adjusting for exports, the model isolates the domestic supply chain where agricultural losses and potential processing residues occur.

FAOSTAT national production figures are used as a structural reference point only. All downstream waste streams are discussed qualitatively due to limited reporting resolution in Lebanon.

2. Waste Stream Dynamics

Stream Characterization

While literature suggests post-harvest apple losses in Lebanon may be substantial, no nationally audited dataset exists. Therefore, waste quantities are discussed as indicative streams rather than fixed values.

National Waste Estimation Model

$$ \text{National Waste} \approx (\text{Production} - \text{Exports}) \times \text{Waste Ratio} $$ $$ \text{National Waste} \approx 214,754 \times 0.40 \approx 85,000 \text{ t (Order of Magnitude)} $$

*The 40% waste ratio is a literature-based estimate for post-harvest loss in developing supply chains, not a measured coefficient.

Agricultural Waste

Field culls, market rejection, and storage losses (scab/rot). Represents a significant share of domestic loss.

Industrial Pomace

Apple pomace is a processing-dependent residue generated only where juice or cider production occurs. In Lebanon, industrial apple processing is limited and geographically concentrated; therefore, pomace availability is structurally constrained and not proportional to total apple waste.

Confidence: Low

Unrecovered Surplus

Apples never entering processing or market. Often abandoned or used as animal feed.

Key Insight

While the gross production volume is high (~235k t), the actual industrial feedstock (pomace) is constrained by the limited processing capacity. Most "waste" likely remains on-farm or is lost in storage (Whole fruit loss), rather than accumulating as factory pomace.

5. Conclusion

The Lebanese apple sector generates significant organic residues, primarily in the form of agricultural culls and storage losses.

However, accurate quantification is hindered by the informal nature of the secondary market. Industrial pomace availability is structurally limited to specific processing clusters and should not be extrapolated from total national production.

"This page intentionally reports apple waste at national scale only and avoids regional or industrial disaggregation due to the absence of audited, disaggregated datasets in Lebanon."
