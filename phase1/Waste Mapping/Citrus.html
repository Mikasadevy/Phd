üîç Executive Summary

This scientific dataset quantifies Citrus (Oranges, Lemons, Grapefruits, Mandarins) waste in Lebanon for the reference year 2023. By reconciling FAOSTAT production data (296,081 t) with export figures (52,098 t), we establish a Net Domestic Supply of ~244,000 tonnes. A verified 50% waste factor applied to this domestic volume yields a Maximum Recoverable Biomass Potential of ~121,992 tonnes/year.

Note: The dataset is designed to identify industrial biomass investment potential (source-generated waste), not to estimate household food waste generation.

2. Waste Generation Methodology

Scientific Scope: "Source-Generated" vs. "Municipal" Waste

This assessment quantifies source-generated citrus biomass waste associated with agricultural and agro-industrial activities, not municipal food waste.

Justification: According to Blominvest (2016), fresh juice producers must be located near orchards to minimize refrigerated transport costs. Furthermore, "inadequate packinghouses" near orchards account for 5-15% losses. Therefore, Production Clusters (South/North) are the scientifically correct proxy for calculating accessible industrial biomass, not urban consumption centers.

A Calculation Logic Flow

  • Input: Total citrus production (FAOSTAT, 2023)
  • Leakage: Deduct fresh exports (UN COMTRADE)
  • Stock: Net domestic citrus supply
  • Generation: Apply composite waste factor
$$ P_{potential} = (P_{prod} - P_{export}) \times 0.50 $$

Processed citrus exports (HS 200830) are downstream products and do not materially affect farm-gate biomass availability.

B Waste Fraction Decomposition (50%)

The 50% factor represents a conservative theoretical maximum for industrial valorization, composed of:

  • Processing Residues (Peel & Pulp) ~35‚Äì40%
  • Packing House Culls / Rejects 5‚Äì15%
