Quantifying the Bio-Based Potential

A rigorous feedstock inventory for sustainable material engineering

Developing scalable bio-based materials requires a reliable, quantified understanding of locally available feedstock.

This dashboard presents a scientifically grounded inventory of Lebanon‚Äôs agricultural and industrial organic waste. Unlike gross production estimates, this model applies a Net Domestic Supply methodology, deducting exports to reveal the true organic load remaining within national borders. This precision is critical for determining the feasibility of industrial applications.

  • Volume Precision: Shifts from theoretical production to export-adjusted waste generation.
  • Industrial Capacity: Includes high-volume streams like Paper & Cardboard (370k t) and Dairy Whey (220k t) alongside agricultural residues.
  • Strategic Zoning: Identifies distinct material belts: the "Carbohydrate Valley" (Bekaa) and the "Fiber Coast" (South).

Net Domestic Supply Logic: This model prioritizes accuracy by excluding exported materials from the waste equation. For example, while national Potato production is ~660k tonnes, approximately 60k tonnes are exported. Waste coefficients are applied only to the remaining ~600k tonne domestic supply to avoid overestimating locally available biomass.

The "Three Belts" of Lebanon

The updated mapping reveals three distinct "Bio-Economic Zones" rather than simple administrative divisions. Each belt offers a unique material profile for sampling and industrial logistics.

Zone A: The Carbohydrate Valley

Bekaa & Baalbek-Hermel: Dominated by high-volume carbohydrate-rich waste streams, including potato residues, cereal straw, and dairy whey (modeled regional concentration). Ideal for rigid boards and starch-based binders.

Zone B: The Fiber Coast

South & Coastal Mt. Lebanon: Dominated by Banana (329k t) and Citrus (122k t). High moisture but immense fibrous volume requiring drying infrastructure.

Zone C: The Urban Mine

Beirut & Maten: The hub for "cleaner" industrial wastes including Paper (370k t) and Textiles (75k t), driven by consumption and light industry.

üõ°Ô∏è Data Defense & Accountability

This dataset prioritizes scientific integrity over false precision. The numbers presented are defensible estimations based on a rigorous three-tier data hierarchy:

  • Tier 1: Verified National Data (FAO/UN)

    All baseline production figures are anchored in official FAOSTAT (2023) and UN Comtrade reports. We do not "invent" production numbers.

  • Tier 2: Scientific Coefficients

    Waste conversion factors (e.g., 1.2 for wheat straw, 4:1 for banana) are derived from peer-reviewed literature (IPCC, Elsevier, MDPI) specific to the Mediterranean context.

  • Tier 3: Logical Regional Proxy

    Where direct regional waste audits are missing, distribution is modelled based on proven proxies (Cultivated Surface Area, Industrial Cluster Maps from IDAL/MoA). These are explicitly labeled as "Estimated Allocations" to maintain transparency.

‚ö†Ô∏è Known Limitations

  • Theoretical Maximums: Values represent the total generated biological material. Recoverable fractions may be lower due to logistical constraints (e.g., collection from smallholders).
  • Qualitative Zones: For wastes like Textiles and Paper, regional data is qualitative (intensity based on population/industry density) rather than quantitative tonnage.
  • Dynamic Factors: Seasonal variations and recent economic shifts may alter industrial throughput, affecting streams like Dairy Whey and Olive Pomace.

Where This Phase Leaves Us

The waste mapping dashboard serves as the quantitative baseline. It confirms that Lebanon possesses sufficient, diverse, and accessible lignocellulosic waste to support a bio-based materials industry, validating the previous MCDA regional selection.
