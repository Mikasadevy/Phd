The authors incorporated 0-10 wt.% wet pomace (dried and ground) into a clay mixture to manufacture ceramic bricks fired at 950¬∞C. ‚ö†Ô∏è Limitations & Risks

üö´

Strict Threshold

The study explicitly states that additions >2.5% result in failure to meet structural standards. This severely limits the volume of waste that can be valorised per brick.

üìâ

Durability Concerns

Authors note that high-pomace bricks are "less durable" than control samples, primarily due to high porosity and water suction.

üéì Actionable Takeaway for PhD

The "Upper Limit" Reference

This paper serves as a definitive reference for the upper limit of wet pomace addition in fired clay bricks. While many waste valorisation papers claim broad success, Cotes Palomino et al. provide a clear failure boundary at 2.5 wt.%.

Next Steps for Research:

  • Focus experimental designs on the 0‚Äì5% range; exploring >10% is likely futile for structural applications.
  • Consider pre-treatment of pomace (e.g., washing or pyrolysis) to mitigate the drastic strength loss observed here.

üå± Sustainability Context

ü´í

Olive Industry Waste

The olive oil industry (vital in Spain/Mediterranean) generates massive quantities of wet pomace ("alperujo"). Currently disposed of in ponds, its valorisation in ceramics offers a pathway to reduce landfill burden while saving virgin clay.

Key Benefit: Porosity generation improves thermal insulation (though at the cost of strength).
