üéì Core Takeaway ‚úì Extracted

The authors synthesized cellulose-based xerogels using cellulose extracted from orange tree waste, incorporating fillers including Olive Pomace (25% and 50%). Results showed that incorporating 50% olive pomace reduced thermal conductivity to 0.022 W/mK compared to 0.0339 W/mK for the pure cellulose control, though density increased from 0.438 to 0.682 g/cm¬≥.

Xerogels often show large shrinkage during drying and collapsed pores; aerogels are expensive to produce (supercritical drying).

Simple, inexpensive method using fillers (Olive Pomace, CNC) and evaporative drying to improve properties.

üéì PhD Context

Relevance Verdict

Core Dataset. This paper provides clean, traceable numeric data for the thermal conductivity of bio-sourced xerogels incorporating olive pomace. It serves as a benchmark for "low-cost" aerogel alternatives.

Actionable Takeaway

Olive pomace up to 50% replacement in cellulose xerogels significantly reduces thermal conductivity (0.022 W/mK) despite increasing density.

üå± Sustainability

Waste Valorization

Utilizes Olive Pomace (agricultural waste) and Orange Tree Cellulose (biomass). Transforms waste into high-value insulation.

Process Efficiency

Avoids energy-intensive supercritical drying used for aerogels. Uses 60¬∞C evaporative drying (though involves solvent exchange with toluene).
